Evidence hierarchy

Use for decisions

  • Predictions whose model training timestamp is before first pitch.
  • Chronological walk-forward comparisons.
  • Resolved appearances only; postponed and no-appearance rows remain ungraded.
  • Confidence intervals and recent-period splits.

Use only for hypothesis generation

  • Historical predictions rescored by a model trained after those games.
  • Backfilled rows whose final batting order was used as a lineup proxy.
  • Backfilled full-season pitcher, park, or sprint context that was not bounded to the game date.
  • Small probability bands, mined cohorts, or Publisher status applied retrospectively.
Point-in-time limitation: 2025 backfills use the final boxscore lineup by default. The current pipeline also obtains some pitcher season context without a historical date bound, while season park factors and sprint speed can contain later-season information. These fields must be rebuilt point-in-time before historical rates can be called fully leakage-safe.
Target limitation: 10,711 no-appearance rows are intentionally ungraded and excluded from training. The saved probability therefore behaves more like P(hit | the hitter appears) than the morning decision's P(hit). A two-stage appearance model, multiplied by hit-given-appearance probability, would align training with the actual pre-lineup choice.

Weighting diagnosis

Likely overweight or unstable

  • Upper raw probability: 75%+ predictions are not calibrated prospectively.
  • Calendar and missingness proxies: month, day of week, rain, and missing park values should not influence a pick without a baseball mechanism.
  • Correlated short-form fields: last-five games, AB, hits, and BA create unstable signs. Replace or supplement them with AB per game and contact-quality windows.
  • Repeated opportunity scoring: PA/game, expected PA, lineup slot, Bob score, safety, discrete bonuses, and rank penalties overlap across model and selection policy.
  • Bob score inside the learned model: useful, but it duplicates many underlying features and can preserve old hand weights.
  • Surface H2H: tiny samples should be shrunk strongly toward pitch-type and platoon expectations.

Likely underweighted

  • Stable hitter contact skill: 500-AB BA and 500-PA K/whiff rates separate current top-five hits from misses.
  • Current-season batting average: it now exists in the candidate table and separates prospective hits, but is absent from the production feature list. Use PA-shrunk season BA rather than a raw early-season rate.
  • Starter quality at the shortlist stage: Stuff+ is more informative prospectively than its current coefficient rank suggests.
  • Current-hand interactions: the model includes both LHP and RHP 500/1500 split values but neither current-season splits nor an explicit interaction selecting today's relevant hand.
  • Appearance probability: lineup certainty and scratch/rest risk need a separate target before official lineups.
  • Recency drift: the recent-half top-one record trails the earlier half, arguing for season or exponential time weights.
  • Calibration: probabilities should be calibrated on held-out recent dates before display.

New free data worth adding

  1. Rolling Statcast contact quality: hitter xBA, hard-hit rate, sweet-spot rate, EV50, and the same contact allowed by the starter. This is the cleanest upgrade from outcome batting average to quality of contact.
  2. Bat-tracking quality: squared-up rate, blasts, competitive-swing contact, bat speed, and swing length. Squared-up contact is especially aligned with a one-hit target.
  3. Pitch-arsenal interaction: weight the hitter's xBA/contact rate against each pitch type by the probable starter's current usage, velocity, movement, and handedness, rather than using one aggregate inferred matchup.
  4. Bullpen availability: pitches thrown and leverage appearances over the prior three days, expected handedness mix, and team off-days. Season bullpen BA does not tell us which relievers are available tonight.
  5. Defense behind the pitcher: team and position-specific Outs Above Average. A single is partly a contact-quality event and partly whether the defense converts the ball.
  6. Lineup certainty and availability: official-lineup timestamp, injury return, rest-day risk, and role changes. Historical final lineups should remain a separate dataset flag, never silently mixed with morning projections.

Primary free references: MLB xBA glossary, Baseball Savant bat tracking, Baseball Savant expected statistics, and MLB Outs Above Average glossary.

Recommended next model cycle

  1. Freeze the current production learned model as the baseline; do not overwrite its historical predictions.
  2. Add a data provenance field for official, projected, recent-usage, or final-boxscore lineup source.
  3. Model P(appearance) separately, then multiply it by P(hit | appearance) for morning rankings.
  4. Build a shadow top-five reranker using stable hitter quality, Stuff+, current-hand interaction, lineup opportunity, and a small stop-count penalty. Train only on earlier dates.
  5. Remove calendar fields and raw rain from the candidate model; retain weather only through game/park context where it has a plausible mechanism.
  6. Fit a recent-date probability calibrator and show calibrated probability beside, not in place of, the raw score during evaluation.
  7. Require at least 50 prospective resolved days before promotion, with predeclared metrics: rank-one hit rate, top-two any-hit rate, longest miss streak, Brier score, and performance by season/month.
Decision today: keep using the learned top five and the daily committee. For the single pick, treat raw ranks one and two as candidates, then explicitly compare 500-AB contact skill, K/whiff rate, lineup opportunity, opposing Stuff+, availability, and current news. Do not automatically choose the higher displayed probability when the gap is small.
